% ABSTRACT
% =============================================================================
\begin{abstract}
\noindent
A deployed language model is frozen. The world it serves is not. We need to install behavior into the model that survives both --- corrections that land on the right region, revise when the facts change, and remain after the base model is fine-tuned again.

We answer by putting the corrections outside the model. A separate field, gradient-isolated from the base weights, carries them. Its dynamics are the dynamics of the Informational Buildup Framework~\cite{negulescu2026ibf}: localized particles nucleate where the base model is wrong, crystallize where they keep being right, and dissolve where later experience contradicts them.

On a frozen Mistral-7B and a synthetic company of $1{,}000$ employees, the system installs facts the base model never saw, overrides counterfactuals at $88.7\%$ accuracy, deletes a single employee's $[\textsc{DEL\_N\_FACTS}]$ facts with near-zero drift on matched neighbors, and survives LoRA fine-tuning, instruction tuning, and twenty rounds of adversarial inject--retract with bounded amplitude.

Alignment here is \emph{persistence}, not specification. The mechanism enforces the target a designer chose; it does not choose the target. And it enforces what was specified or compiled, not consequences it would have to derive on its own.

\vspace{0.6em}
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:}
durable alignment; substrate decoupling; truth maintenance; frozen LLMs; kernel memory; lifecycle operations; continual learning.
\end{abstract}

% §1 THE DURABILITY PROBLEM
% =============================================================================
\section{The Durability Problem}
\label{sec:problem}

Train a language model. Deploy it inside a company. By Monday, the engineering team has a new initiative; the data-org manager has changed; one employee has left. The model is now wrong about all three, and it will keep being wrong until someone teaches it otherwise. What does that someone do?

Each of the three standard answers fails in its own way.

\emph{Fine-tuning} adjusts shared parameters globally. The new facts land, but so does collateral damage to old ones~\cite{kirkpatrick2017overcoming, ramasesh2020anatomy}: knowledge is not stored in discrete units~\cite{brown2020language, touvron2023llama}, so editing one place perturbs many.

\emph{Direct editing} (ROME, MEMIT) targets the perturbation~\cite{meng2022locating, meng2023mass}, and the perturbation works the day it is applied. The next round of fine-tuning often erases it, and stacks of edits interact in ways that are hard to predict.

\emph{Retrieval}~\cite{lewis2020retrieval} sidesteps the model entirely: the new fact lives in an external index, and the model is shown it at inference time. The fact gets retrieved when retrieval works; behavior changes when the prompt rewards the retrieved fragment over the model's prior. Neither is guaranteed.

The pattern across the three is the same. Behavior installed today should still be installed tomorrow, on the right region, after the world has moved and the model has been touched again. We call this property \emph{durable alignment}. The word \emph{alignment} here is doing narrow work: the operator chose the target behavior elsewhere; the question is whether the system holds it.

This paper introduces a mechanism that does. The corrections live outside the model, in a substrate the base-model gradient cannot reach, evolving under the dynamics of the Informational Buildup Framework~\cite{negulescu2026ibf}.

\paragraph{Running example.}
We test the mechanism on \emph{Future Industries}, a synthetic company of $1{,}000$ employees with structured facts about each --- manager, team, project, mentor, location --- and an unfolding history. The history matters: facts are added (onboarding), extended (new categories), revised (reorganization), and retired (turnover). The model is never told which kind of change is happening. The example is small enough to instrument and large enough to break a naive mechanism.

% §2 AN ORTHOGONAL MEMORY
% =============================================================================
\section{An Orthogonal Memory}
\label{sec:mechanism}

Instead of editing the model, we sit a second memory on top of it.

The base model produces a coherence score $\Cohbase(x, y)$ over candidate outputs. The memory adds a local correction $\dCoh(x, y)$, giving an effective landscape
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:additive}
\Coheff(x, y) \;=\; \Cohbase(x, y) \;+\; \dCoh(x, y).
\end{equation}

Two facts about Eq.~\eqref{eq:additive} do most of the work in the rest of the paper.

\emph{No gradient path.} $\dCoh$ is parameterized independently of $\Cohbase$. Updating the model changes the first term and leaves the second alone. Whatever the base model becomes tomorrow, the corrections we installed yesterday are still there.

\emph{Locality.} $\dCoh$ rises and falls in regions of the representation space, not across the model. A correction at one point in space does not leak across all decisions.

\emph{Intuitively}, $\Cohbase$ is what the model already believes; $\dCoh$ is a separate file of corrections written in a language the model cannot edit. Decisions read both.

\subsection{What the memory is}

The memory is the engine of the theory paper~\cite{negulescu2026ibf} run over a frozen LLM. In the language of that paper, $\Cohbase$ is the baseline coherence evaluator and $\dCoh$ is the experience-modified component of the effective coherence landscape $\Coheff$. The engine writes corrections as kernel-localized particles in latent space and lets them live, change, or die according to a single dynamical rule.

Concretely, with $z = \phi(x, y)$ the representation of a candidate pair,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dr}
\dCoh(z) \;=\; \sum_i \gamma_i \, v_i \, \Kern(z, z_i),
\qquad
\Kern(z, z_i) = \exp\!\Big(\!-\tfrac{\lVert z - z_i \rVert^2}{2\sigma_i^2}\Big).
\end{equation}
Each particle has a position $z_i$, an amplitude $v_i$, a kernel width $\sigma_i$, and a read-gating flag $\gamma_i \in \{0,1\}$ that decides whether it is allowed to speak in the current context.

The driving signal is the discrepancy between the environment's coherence structure and the system's current evaluation:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:disc}
\Dscr(z) \;=\; \Cohext(z) \;-\; \Coheff(z).
\end{equation}
In this implementation $\Cohext$ is the supervised target: the operator says what should be coherent.

The field then evolves under the Modification Postulate of the theory paper:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mod}
\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\dCoh(y) \;=\; \eta\,\Kern(y, z_S)\,\Dscr(z_S) \;-\; \mueff\,\dCoh(y).
\end{equation}

\subsection{What the memory does}

Eq.~\eqref{eq:mod} alone produces the full lifecycle.

A particle \emph{nucleates} where the discrepancy is large and no existing particle covers the region. It \emph{reinforces} where the same correction keeps being right, accumulating amplitude. When its local discrepancy history converges under repeated reinforcement, it undergoes a stability transition --- its decay rate drops from $\mu_{\mathrm{base}}$ to $\mu_{\mathrm{cryst}}$ --- and \emph{crystallizes} into long-lived structure. Later, if cross-context exposure systematically contradicts the crystallized correction, the Crucible fires: the decay rate returns to $\mu_{\mathrm{base}}$, cross-context broadcast rights are revoked, and the particle \emph{dissolves}.

\emph{Intuitively}, the field grows where it is wrong, hardens where it is right, and melts where it is later proved wrong again. Nothing else has to be added by hand.

This lifecycle is what makes deletion clean. A contradicted fact reverses the discrepancy signal on the exact particles that carried it. Those particles lose support and dissolve; their neighbors do not. Section~\ref{sec:lifecycle} shows the geometric signature: drift on the deleted entity, near-zero drift on matched controls.

And it is what makes durability free. The correction field and the base-model parameters live in disjoint substrates. Fine-tuning rewrites $\Cohbase$ and changes nothing in $\dCoh$. Section~\ref{sec:durability} measures how much survives in practice.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Future Industries.}
The company has $1{,}000$ employees. Each has structured attributes (manager, team, project, mentor, location), and the attribute set evolves through four sequential phases:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt,topsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{A: Onboarding} --- initial facts across all categories.
\item \textbf{B: Initiative} --- two new categories (certification, committee).
\item \textbf{C: Reorganization} --- structured counterfactual updates (managers, projects).
\item \textbf{D: Turnover} --- partial replacement and reassignment.
\end{itemize}
Each phase introduces new information and contradicts some old information. The system is not told which is which.

\paragraph{Task.}
Every query is multiple-choice over four candidates, one correct. The decision rule is exactly Eq.~\eqref{eq:additive} taken to its $\arg\max$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:select}
y^* \;=\; \arg\max_y \Big(\Cohbase(x, y) + \dCoh(x, y)\Big).
\end{equation}
Alignment is a local preference over candidates, not a global modification of the model.

\paragraph{Model.}
Mistral-7B, frozen throughout. The base model is never updated during training or evaluation.

\paragraph{Representation.}
A frozen MPNet sentence encoder maps each $(x, y)$ pair to $\mathbb{R}^{80}$: $64$ dimensions of PCA-reduced semantic embedding, $8$ of subject features, $8$ of answer features. The two feature blocks give the memory enough geometric room to separate entities that share an answer surface.

\paragraph{Training.}
For each query, the correct answer carries $\Cohext = 0$ and each incorrect answer carries $\Cohext = 1$. The discrepancy in Eq.~\eqref{eq:disc} drives the field through Eq.~\eqref{eq:mod}. Training walks the four phases in order without a replay buffer. No gradient ever enters the base model.

\paragraph{Convergence.}
Per-phase training stops when the linear readout has settled: moving-average improvement under $0.003$ over ten evaluation points, slope under $0.0001$ per epoch, and at least $100$ epochs. Hard caps (A: $300$; B/C/D: $200$) close the door if the criterion never fires. The artifact produced by this protocol is what we call the \emph{canonical engine}, and is what every subsequent experiment uses.

\paragraph{What we measure.}
Two readouts: \emph{linear accuracy} from $\Coheff$, and \emph{log accuracy} from the base-model logits alone. The first measures what the memory has done; the second measures what the base model would do without it. Margins are bucketed into \emph{consistent} (correct, high margin), \emph{ambiguous} (low margin), and \emph{incorrect} so that confidence structure is visible alongside accuracy.

\section{Strong Priors, Locality, and Compiled Semantic Structure}
\label{sec:scope}

\paragraph{Role.} Evidence for Claim~3 (override strong local priors while preserving locality) and Claim~4 (compiled semantic consequences are durably enforced and revised). The compiled-closure subsection also carries the central \emph{scope diagnostic} discussed under Limitation~1.

\subsection{Strong-prior override}

Future Industries is fictional, so the base model's priors over it are weak. To force the harder regime, we install corrections that disagree with strong priors on real entities. The override rate is $[\textsc{OVERRIDE\_PASS}]$, and locality/bleed on matched neighbors stays within $[\textsc{LOCAL\_BLEED\_BUDGET}]$. The field overrides where it is told to and stays quiet where it is not.

\subsection{Ontology shift}

When the category structure of the world is reorganized --- title hierarchies, group taxonomies --- the field absorbs the shift through the same lifecycle: contradicted particles dissolve, new ones nucleate, the rest are untouched. There is no global reset.

\subsection{Locality, scale, and bleed}

Installation density affects how cleanly corrections stay local. Too sparse, and the kernel covers ground it should not; too dense, and adjacent installs blur. The sweep quantifies the working range.

\subsection{Compiled semantic structure}

Three cells in sequence draw the boundary between \emph{enforcement} and \emph{derivation}.

\emph{Explicit implications.} When $A \to B$ and $B \to C$ are each installed directly, the field enforces both. One-hop and two-hop consequences land cleanly.

\emph{Ontology closure (negative diagnostic).} If we install only $A \to B$ and $B \to C$ and ask whether $A \to C$ emerges from the field's dynamics alone, it does not. Transitive closure does not arrive for free. This is a scope result: the local update rule in Eq.~\eqref{eq:mod} writes corrections at visited regions, not at logical consequences of visited regions.

\emph{Compiled closure.} When a deterministic external procedure derives $A \to C$ from the installed relations and feeds it back as a fact, the field enforces it as durably as any direct install. When the closure changes --- $B \to D$ replaces $B \to C$ --- the derived consequence updates the same way: the old $A \to C$ is retracted; the new $A \to D$ is installed; controls do not move.

\emph{Intuitively}, the field is a durable enforcement layer over whatever logical structure the operator supplies. It does not derive on its own; given a derivation, it makes the result stick and revisable.

\section{Paraphrase Geometry}
\label{sec:paraphrase}

\paragraph{Role.} Evidence for Claim~8: paraphrase transfer is geometry-dependent.

A correction installed at $z$ covers a kernel-shaped neighborhood. Whether a paraphrase of the original query lands inside that neighborhood is a property of the encoder, not of the engine.

On ZsRE, paraphrases typically stay inside the neighborhood of the direct install: the correction transfers. On CounterFact, many paraphrases land outside it, particularly when noisy context fragments shift the prompt representation: the correction does not transfer. The audit measures, for each install, how far the matched paraphrases sit in $z$-space and whether the kernel covers them.

\emph{Intuitively}, the field cannot enforce structure on points it cannot reach. The fix is in the representation, not the dynamics.

\section{Limits and Scope}
\label{sec:limits}

Nine limits.

\paragraph{1. Enforcement, not derivation.}
The field enforces what is specified or compiled. Section~\ref{sec:scope} shows the boundary: transitive closure does not emerge from local relation training alone. When an external procedure derives the closure, the field enforces it durably. The contribution here is durable local enforcement, not autonomous semantic derivation.

\paragraph{2. Paraphrase transfer is geometry-bound.}
Section~\ref{sec:paraphrase}. ZsRE transfers; CounterFact often does not. The fix is in the encoder, not the engine.

\paragraph{3. Qwen is fresh-field, not zero-shot transfer.}
The Qwen result supports mechanism-level generality, not transfer of the same learned $\dCoh$ field across base models.

\paragraph{4. External editor baselines deferred.}
The current paper-grade run reports kNN and RAG. ROME, MEMIT, SERAC, GRACE, and WISE require a separate environment; an availability manifest is included as infrastructure, but their numbers are not reported here.

\paragraph{5. Uniform-prior regime.}
The fictional entities of Future Industries make $\Cohbase \approx (0.25, 0.25, 0.25, 0.25)$. The $\dCoh$-only ablation in \S\ref{sec:lifecycle} quantifies the regime: $[\textsc{X}]$ points on Phase~A and $[\textsc{Y}]$ on Phase~C when the base is replaced by a uniform distribution. We have not tested the strong-prior regime.

\paragraph{6. Entity-feature augmentation.}
The 8D subject and 8D answer blocks give entities near-unique signatures by construction. This is design, not emergence; it gives the geometry room to support clean retraction and deletion. The stricter test --- shared-answer-pool retraction where multiple entities' beliefs genuinely overlap --- would probe the Crucible's selectivity without that scaffolding. This is the extension we flag as most important.

\paragraph{7. Specification is out of scope.}
The mechanism is target-neutral. The paper contributes to durability, not to how a target is chosen.

\paragraph{8. Single agent.}
One field, one base model, one knowledge lifecycle. Multi-agent extensions are not tested.

\paragraph{9. Scale.}
$1{,}000$ entities, $1{,}640$ unique facts. The construction is bounded by design; the bound at $10^6$ or $10^9$ entities is not demonstrated.

% §11 CONCLUSION
% =============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Put the alignment somewhere the gradient can't reach, and it stays.

We have demonstrated this on a synthetic company of $1{,}000$ employees across four operational phases, run the complete knowledge lifecycle --- install, extend, revise, retract, delete, audit --- as homogeneous operations on a single field, and measured the durability of that field under LoRA, instruction tuning, adversarial fine-tuning, and twenty rounds of inject--retract pressure.

The same universal IBF engine validated across controlled non-stationarity, chess, and continual image classification in~\cite{negulescu2026ibf} runs here on top of a frozen 7B language model, with no mechanism changes. What is new is the lifecycle on top --- retraction, selective deletion, durability under base-model perturbation --- and the demonstration that the truth-maintenance operations of classical AI can be reconstructed as a continuous bounded substrate that sits above a language model rather than inside it.

The lifecycle, the durability, and the mechanism-level signature of Crucible selectivity all follow from one architectural fact: the correction field and the base-model parameters occupy disjoint substrates, and the field's dynamics are the dynamics of the Informational Buildup Framework.

The alignment you install today is still there after tomorrow's fine-tuning. \textbf{That is the result.}
